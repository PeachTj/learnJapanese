# Learn how to use conversational Japanese and how to read calculation formulas

**The Focus of This Lesson Is Contractions We Use in Conversational Japanese.**

**I have to get up early, and my hands get dirty-it's really not easy, you know**

**早く起きなくちゃいけないし、手、汚くなっちゃうし、ほんと、楽じゃないっすね。**

***Hayaku okinakucha ikenai shi, te, kitanaku nacchau shi, honto rakuja nai ssu ne.***

## **Contracted Forms We Often Use in Conversation**

**～ている becomes ～てる**

"I'm waiting”

待っ**ている** [*mat**te iru***] → 待っ**てる**[*mat**te ru***]

**～ていく[-teiku]  becomes  ～てく[-teku]**
"Are you going to take this?”

これ、もっ**て行く**？[*Kore mot**te iku***?] → これ、もっ**てく**？[*Kore mot**teku**?*]

**～ておく[-*te oku*]  becomes ～とく[-*to ku*]**
"I bought it in advance."

買っ**ておいた** [*kat**te oita***] → 買っ**といた** [*kat**toita***]

**～てはいけない becomes ～ちゃいけない**

“You shouldn't enter."

入っ**ては**いけない [*hait**te wa** ikenai*] → 入っ**ちゃ**いけない [*haic**cha** ikenai*]"

**～なくてはいけない becomes ～なくちゃいけない**

"I have to go.”

行かなく**ては**いけない [*ikanaku**te wa** ikenai*] → 行かなくちゃいけない[*ikanaku**cha** ikenai*]

**～なければいけない becomes ～なきゃいけない**

"I have to go.”

行か**なければ**いけない [*ika**nakereba** ikenai*] → 行か**なきゃ**いけない [*ika**nakya*** *ikenai*]

**～てしまう becomes ～ちゃう**

"to completely drink”

飲ん**でしまう**[*non**de shimau***] → 飲ん**じゃう** [*non**jau***]

**です becomes っす**

"It's one hundred yen.”

100円**です**。[*100 en **desu***] → 100円**っす**。 [*100en **ssu***.]

**Particle Omission**

In informal conversation, we often omit the particles *o*, *ga*, *e*, and *ni* (for destinations) and *wa* (for topics) from a sentence.

## **How to Read Calculation Formulas in Japanese**

**How to Read Decimals**

We read a decimal point as *ten* (点) "dot" in Japanese. After the decimal point, we read the numbers one by one.

| **Number** | ***Japanese*** |
| --- | --- |
| 0.1 | *rei ten ichi*/*zero ten ichi* |
| 1.4 | *itten yon*/*ichi ten yon* |
| 2.56 | *ni ten go roku*/*nī ten go roku* |
| 3.0 | *san ten zero*/*san ten rei* |
| 4.1 | *yon ten ichi* |
| 5.83 | *go ten hachi san*/*gō ten hachi san* |
| 6.1 | *roku ten ichi* |
| 7.2 | *nana ten ni* |
| 8.9 | *hatten kyū*/*hachi ten kyū* |
| 9.99 | *kyū ten kyū kyū* |
| 10.1 | *jutten ichi* |

**How to Read Common Fractions**

We read common fractions as [(denominator)*bun no* (numerator)].

**For Example:**

1. 1/2 = *ni bun no ichi* (二分の一)
2. 1/3 = *san bun no ichi* (三分の一)
3. 3/5 = *go bun no san* (五分の三)

**How to Read Mathematical Symbols**

| **Symbol** | ***Kanji*** | ***Reading*** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ＋ | 足す | *tasu* |
| － | 引く | *hiku* |
| × | 掛ける | *kakeru* |
| ÷ | 割る | *waru* |
- = We read "equals" as *wa* (は) or *ikōru* (イコール).
- We read －, meaning "below zero," as *mainasu* (マイナス)　cf. －４ = *mainasu yon* (マイナス4)

### **ey Vocabulary & Phrases**

**■ *yaoya* (八百屋) "vegetable shop," "seller of vegetables"**

*Yaoya* is a "vegetable shop" or "greengrocer." This word has a special reading, so be careful.

Words like this that have special Kanji readings are called *jukujikun* (熟字訓).

**■ *o shiteiru* "occupation"**

The verb *suru* has the meanings of "to play a role of...," "to run (a business)," or "to make a living by..." You can refer to what someone does for living by adding *o shiteiru* to the name of an occupation.

私は教師をしている。

**■*ni-wari* (二割) "twenty percent"**

*Wari* is a unit for "ten percent." So *ichi-wari* is "ten percent," *san-wari* is "thirty percent," and

*jū-wari* is "one hundred percent," and so forth.

ニ割引き

**■*han-gaku* (半額) "half price"***Han* means "half," and *gaku* means "sum or amount (of money)." *Zengaku* (全額) means "full amount."

**■*chōdai*(ちょうだい) "give me..."**

*Chōdai* itself is a noun that means "receiving." Customers often use [noun/pronoun]＋

*chōdai* at shops or restaurants when asking for something. It sounds much friendlier and informal than [noun/pronoun]＋*kudasai*
